# Medication Safety: What You Need to Know

**Published:** April 21, 2026  
**Author:** Langley Park Pharmacy  
**Reading Time:** 6 minutes

---

Taking medications safely might seem straightforward—just follow the label, right? The reality is a bit more complex. Medication mistakes happen more often than you might think, and many are completely preventable. Understanding how to take your medications safely is one of the most important things you can do for your health.

## Common Medication Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### 1. Not Taking Medications as Prescribed

**The mistake:** Taking less medication to "stretch it out," stopping early because you feel better, or taking more because you want faster results.

**Why it's risky:** Your dose is carefully calculated for your condition. Too little won't be effective; too much can cause dangerous side effects.

**The fix:** Take exactly as prescribed. If you think the dose isn't working, talk to your doctor—don't adjust on your own.

### 2. Mixing Up Similar-Looking Medications

**The mistake:** Grabbing the wrong bottle in the dark or confusing medications that look alike.

**Why it's risky:** Even small mix-ups can have serious consequences.

**The fix:** Use a pill organizer with labels. Check labels carefully each time. Ask your pharmacist to use different-looking bottles for your medications if possible.

### 3. Forgetting to Take Medications

**The mistake:** Missing doses because you forget or lose track of what you've already taken.

**Why it's risky:** Missed doses reduce effectiveness of treatment.

**The fix:** 
- Use a pill organizer (daily or weekly)
- Set phone reminders or alarms
- Link taking medications to a daily habit (morning coffee, brushing teeth)
- Ask your pharmacist about automatic refill programs

### 4. Taking Medications at the Wrong Time

**The mistake:** Taking medications "whenever you remember" instead of following the schedule.

**Why it's risky:** Some medications work best at specific times. Others need to be spaced apart. Timing affects absorption and effectiveness.

**The fix:** Read labels carefully. "Twice daily" doesn't mean morning and night—it means every 12 hours. Set alarms if needed.

### 5. Not Checking for Drug Interactions

**The mistake:** Taking multiple medications without knowing they interact with each other.

**Why it's risky:** Drug interactions can reduce effectiveness, increase side effects, or cause dangerous reactions.

**The fix:** Tell your pharmacist about ALL medications, supplements, and herbal products you take. This includes over-the-counter items!

## 5 Essential Rules for Medication Safety

### Rule 1: Know Your Medications

For each medication you take, know:
- What it's for (the condition it treats)
- How much to take (the dose)
- When to take it (frequency and time of day)
- How to take it (with food? with water? swallow whole?)
- Common side effects
- What to do if you miss a dose

Ask your pharmacist to explain all of this. It's their job, and they're happy to help.

### Rule 2: Read the Label Every Time

Yes, every single time. Even if you've taken the medication a hundred times. Things change:
- Strength might be different
- Instructions might be updated
- Your pharmacy might have changed the physical appearance
- It's easy to grab the wrong bottle when drowsy

**Always double-check the label.**

### Rule 3: Understand Possible Interactions

**Drug-drug interactions:** When two medications affect each other
- Some combinations increase side effects
- Some reduce effectiveness
- Some can be dangerous together

**Drug-food interactions:** When what you eat affects medications
- Some medications work better with food; others need an empty stomach
- Grapefruit juice interacts with many drugs
- Alcohol interacts with many medications

**Drug-supplement interactions:** Over-the-counter items matter too
- St. John's Wort reduces effectiveness of birth control
- Some supplements increase bleeding risk
- Vitamins and minerals can interfere with absorption

**Always tell your pharmacist everything you take.**

### Rule 4: Store Medications Properly

Improper storage reduces effectiveness and can make medications unsafe:
- Store at room temperature (unless otherwise directed)
- Keep away from heat, moisture, and direct sunlight
- Avoid bathrooms (too humid)
- Don't store in the kitchen near the stove
- Keep away from children and pets
- Don't store in the car (temperature fluctuations)

Check labels for specific storage instructions.

### Rule 5: Know Your Medical History

Your pharmacist needs to know:
- All current medical conditions
- Past medical problems
- Allergies (including food and drug allergies)
- Pregnancy status (if applicable)
- Liver or kidney disease
- Heart problems
- Other factors that might affect medications

Be honest about everything. Your pharmacist isn't judging—they just need accurate information to keep you safe.

## Red Flags: When to Call Your Pharmacist

Contact your pharmacist immediately if you experience:

- **Unexpected side effects** – New symptoms after starting a medication
- **Allergic reactions** – Rash, swelling, difficulty breathing
- **Severe side effects** – Chest pain, vision changes, confusion, severe headache
- **Unusual bleeding or bruising**
- **Persistent nausea or vomiting**
- **Difficulty urinating or changes in urination**
- **Yellowing of skin or eyes** (liver issues)
- **Swelling in legs or feet**
- **Persistent dizziness**

Don't wait—call immediately for any concerning symptoms.

## Questions to Ask Your Pharmacist

When you pick up a new medication, ask:

1. **"What is this medication for?"** (confirm your condition)
2. **"How much should I take?"** (confirm the dose)
3. **"When should I take it?"** (morning? evening? with food?)
4. **"Are there foods or drinks I should avoid?"**
5. **"Will this interact with my other medications?"** (share your full list)
6. **"What are the most common side effects?"**
7. **"What side effects should I report to my doctor?"**
8. **"What should I do if I miss a dose?"**
9. **"Can I take this with over-the-counter medications?"**
10. **"Where should I store this?"**

Good pharmacists expect these questions and appreciate them. They show you're engaged in your own care.

## Keeping Organized: A Practical System

Create a simple medication tracking system:

**Medication List (keep updated):**
- Medication name
- Dose
- Frequency
- What it treats
- Side effects to watch for

**Weekly Pill Organizer:**
- Labeled by day and time
- Fill weekly (or ask pharmacy for pre-filled packs)
- Easy to see if you missed a dose

**Storage:**
- Cool, dry place
- Original bottles (labels are important)
- Clearly marked and organized
- Out of reach of children/pets

**Documentation:**
- Keep a list of all medications on your phone
- Share with family members
- Update when anything changes

## Special Considerations

### For Older Adults
- Take time to review medications with your pharmacist
- Watch for drug-disease interactions
- Be aware of increased sensitivity to side effects
- Use pill organizers
- Have regular medication reviews

### For Caregivers
- Keep updated medication lists
- Help with organization and reminders
- Watch for side effects
- Attend appointments when possible
- Ask questions on behalf of the person you care for

### For Chronic Conditions
- Consistency is crucial
- Set up automatic refills
- Don't skip doses even if feeling better
- Regular check-ins with your doctor and pharmacist
- Monitor for side effects

## The Bottom Line

Medication safety isn't complicated, but it does require attention and organization. By following these guidelines, you'll maximize the benefits of your medications while minimizing risks.

Your pharmacist is your best resource. They're medication experts dedicated to your safety. Use them. Ask questions. Keep them updated. Work together as a team for your best health.
